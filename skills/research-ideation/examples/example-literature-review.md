# Example Literature Review: Municipal Tax Incentives and Firm Entry

## Research Question

Do municipal tax incentives increase firm entry, and through which margins do they operate?

## Why This Literature Matters

This literature matters because municipal tax incentives are often justified as tools for local economic development, yet the relevant margin is not always clear. A rise in observed firm entry may reflect genuine net creation, cross-municipality relocation, short-run timing shifts, or differential survival rather than sustained local growth. The literature therefore needs to be organized not only around whether incentives “work,” but also around what outcome is identified, over what horizon, and under what institutional setting.

## Literature Map

### 1. Classic Policy Incidence and Local Public Finance

- focus: how local tax policy shapes firm location, investment, and tax-base allocation
- what this literature establishes: local fiscal policy can affect location incentives, especially when firms compare jurisdictions with different tax prices or expenditure bundles
- relevance for this project: provides the conceptual basis for why municipal tax incentives may affect entry decisions
- main limitation for this project: much of this literature is stronger on location incentives in general than on entry-specific margins or persistence

### 2. Place-Based Policy and Firm Location

- focus: special economic zones, enterprise zones, local subsidy programs, tax relief, and place-based incentives
- what this literature establishes: place-based incentives can shift business activity toward treated places, but estimated effects often depend on design, policy scale, and the possibility of displacement
- common identification families:
  - staggered policy adoption
  - border discontinuities
  - rule-based program eligibility
- relevance for this project: directly informs how municipal incentives may affect local entry
- main limitation for this project: many studies cannot cleanly distinguish net new activity from spatial reallocation across nearby jurisdictions

### 3. Administrative Data Studies of Firm Dynamics

- focus: business registry data, employer-employee records, municipal finance data, and linked administrative panels
- what this literature establishes: administrative data can track entry, exit, survival, and sometimes relocation or employment outcomes with much greater precision than survey-based studies
- relevance for this project: shows which outcomes, timing structures, and sample restrictions are empirically feasible
- main limitation for this project: data-rich studies do not automatically solve identification problems, especially when policy timing is endogenous or nearby spillovers are strong

## Identification Buckets and What They Help Identify

### DID / Event Study

- useful when adoption timing differs across municipalities
- what it can identify: average changes in firm entry around incentive adoption, especially under parallel-trends-type assumptions
- key concern: differential pre-trends, selective timing, and concurrent municipal policy changes
- literature implication: good for dynamic policy timing analysis, but credibility depends heavily on treatment timing and comparison-group construction

### RDD / Eligibility Thresholds

- useful when incentive access depends on a cutoff
- what it can identify: local treatment effects near the eligibility boundary
- key concern: manipulation around the threshold and limited external validity
- literature implication: often stronger on local causal identification, but narrower in scope

### Border Designs

- useful when adjacent municipalities face different incentive regimes
- what it can identify: location responses under close geographic comparison
- key concern: sorting, spillovers, and concurrent local policy differences
- literature implication: especially useful for testing spatial displacement, but interpretation depends on how cleanly neighboring places differ only in incentive exposure

## Main Open Questions

- are incentives increasing net firm entry, or mainly relocating firms across nearby municipalities?
- do incentives affect true new entrants differently from incumbent firms, branch establishments, or formalizing firms?
- are short-run entry gains persistent, or do they fade once temporary tax advantages expire?
- how large are displacement effects relative to headline treated-area gains?
- do policy effects differ by sector, firm size, or baseline local economic conditions?

## This Project's Likely Contribution

Relative to the existing literature, this project is likely to contribute in three ways:

1. it links municipal policy timing to administrative firm records in order to study entry on a more precise margin than broad local economic aggregates;
2. it compares immediate entry responses with persistence outcomes, helping distinguish short-run attraction from longer-run local firm formation;
3. it explicitly considers whether observed gains reflect net new activity or spatial displacement across neighboring municipalities.

## Example Synthesis Paragraph

A concise literature review paragraph in this area might read as follows:

Research on local fiscal policy and place-based incentives suggests that tax differentials can shape firm location decisions, but the margin of adjustment is often unclear. Classic work in local public finance establishes that firms respond to jurisdiction-level policy incentives, while more recent studies of place-based programs examine whether special zones, tax relief, or local subsidies attract business activity into treated areas. However, existing evidence does not always distinguish genuine firm creation from cross-jurisdiction relocation, and many studies remain limited in their ability to assess persistence beyond the short run. Administrative-data studies improve measurement of entry and survival, but identification still depends on the credibility of policy timing, eligibility rules, or border comparisons. This project builds on that literature by combining municipal policy timing with firm-level administrative records to distinguish headline entry responses from persistence and potential spatial displacement.
