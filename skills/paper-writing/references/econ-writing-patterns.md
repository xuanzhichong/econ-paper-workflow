# Economics and Agricultural Economics Writing Patterns

Use these as direction-of-travel templates, not boilerplate to copy blindly.

These patterns are for empirical economics and agricultural economics writing.  
They are meant to help with paragraph construction, claim discipline, and paper flow, not to produce formulaic prose.

A good writing pattern should help the paper sound:

- clear
- disciplined
- design-aware
- proportionate to the evidence

---

## Introduction Patterns

Use these to introduce the question, motivate the paper, and position it in the literature.

- "This paper studies whether ..."
- "We focus on ... because ..."
- "The question matters because ..."
- "Existing work shows that ..., but it remains unclear whether ..."
- "A central challenge in this literature is that ..."
- "Our empirical strategy exploits ..."
- "We use ... data to examine ..."
- "The main finding is that ..."
- "Relative to the existing literature, this paper contributes by ..."
- "The paper makes three main contributions."
- "The remainder of the paper is organized as follows."

### Useful introduction logic

- question
- why it matters
- what the literature already knows
- what remains unresolved
- design and data
- main finding
- contribution
- roadmap

---

## Institutional Background / Context Patterns

Use these to explain the setting, policy, or market environment without turning the section into a general background essay.

- "The policy was introduced in ..."
- "Eligibility depended on ..."
- "Implementation occurred in stages across ..."
- "This setting is useful because it generates variation in ..."
- "The institutional feature most relevant for identification is ..."
- "The policy altered incentives through ..."
- "These features make ... a useful setting for studying ..."

### Discipline reminder

- include only the context needed to understand the design
- separate policy facts from interpretation

---

## Data Patterns

Use these to describe sources, sample construction, and variables clearly.

- "Our main data come from ..."
- "The unit of observation is ..."
- "The sample covers ... from ... to ..."
- "We restrict the sample to ..."
- "The final estimation sample includes ..."
- "Treatment is defined as ..."
- "Our main outcome measures ..."
- "Control variables include ..."
- "Appendix Table A1 reports the sample construction in detail."
- "All monetary variables are deflated to ..."
- "Unless otherwise noted, all regressions use the same estimation sample."

### Data-discipline phrases

- "To maintain comparability across specifications, we use a harmonized sample ..."
- "Observations are excluded when ..."
- "Missing values are handled as follows ..."

---

## Empirical Strategy Patterns

Use these to state the design, assumptions, and threats explicitly.

- "Identification comes from ..."
- "The identifying variation arises from ..."
- "We estimate the following specification ..."
- "The key assumption is that ..."
- "A threat to this design is ..."
- "We address this concern by ..."
- "Our preferred specification includes ..."
- "Standard errors are clustered at the ... level because ..."
- "The event-study specification allows us to examine ..."
- "The omitted period is ..."
- "The first-stage results show that ..."
- "The IV estimate should be interpreted as ..."

### Good strategy-writing moves

- "This design compares ... before and after ... relative to ..."
- "Under the maintained identifying assumption, the coefficient on ... captures ..."
- "We interpret these estimates as ..."

---

## Results Patterns

Use these to write around tables and figures rather than around vague claims.

- "Table X reports the baseline specification."
- "Column 1 of Table X reports the baseline specification."
- "Columns 2–4 add ..."
- "The coefficient on ... is positive and statistically significant."
- "The estimate implies that ..."
- "The magnitude is economically meaningful because ..."
- "Relative to the pre-treatment mean, this corresponds to ..."
- "The coefficient remains stable when ..."
- "The estimate becomes smaller once ... is included, but remains ..."
- "Figure X presents the event-study estimates around ..."
- "The pre-treatment coefficients provide no strong evidence of ..."
- "Post-treatment coefficients become larger over time, suggesting that ..."

### Results-discipline phrases

- "These estimates are consistent with ..."
- "This pattern is not driven solely by ..."
- "The substantive conclusion is unchanged."

---

## Robustness Patterns

Use these when linking checks to specific design threats.

- "To address the concern that ..., we ..."
- "Table X and Appendix Table AX report alternative ..."
- "These checks reduce the concern that ..."
- "The estimate remains similar in sign and magnitude ..."
- "Precision falls somewhat, but the qualitative conclusion is unchanged."
- "The result is not driven by ..."
- "Alternative treatment definitions yield similar estimates."
- "Excluding ... leaves the main pattern intact."

### Robustness-discipline reminder

Do not write:

- "The results are robust to many specifications."

Prefer:

- "These checks reduce the concern that the baseline estimate is driven by treatment coding / sample composition / one particular inference choice."

---

## Heterogeneity Patterns

Use these when subgroup results are theory-motivated and help interpret the main effect.

- "We next examine whether the effect varies by ..."
- "Table X reports heterogeneity by ..."
- "The estimates suggest stronger effects in ..."
- "This pattern is consistent with the idea that ..."
- "The subgroup differences are economically meaningful because ..."
- "These heterogeneity results should be interpreted as supportive rather than definitive evidence."

### Heterogeneity-discipline reminder

- do not turn subgroup patterns into the main contribution unless they truly are central
- do not present ad hoc splits as if they were theory-driven

---

## Mechanism Patterns

Use these when presenting supporting evidence on channels.

- "We examine several potential mechanisms through which ..."
- "The mechanism evidence is consistent with ..."
- "These findings suggest that ..."
- "One possible interpretation is that ..."
- "This pattern is consistent with the mechanism operating through ..."
- "The mechanism evidence is suggestive rather than definitive."
- "Because these mechanism outcomes are not separately identified, the evidence should be interpreted cautiously."

### Mechanism-discipline reminder

- mechanism evidence should usually be weaker than the main effect claim
- do not write mechanism results as structural proof unless the design supports that

---

## Caution Patterns

Use these whenever the evidence is real but the design or scope does not justify stronger language.

- "These results should be interpreted as ..."
- "The mechanism evidence is suggestive rather than definitive."
- "External validity may be limited to ..."
- "The findings are consistent with ..., although ..."
- "This evidence reduces, but does not eliminate, the concern that ..."
- "We cannot rule out ..."
- "The estimate should therefore be interpreted with caution."
- "The results are best understood as evidence on ... rather than ..."

---

## Conclusion Patterns

Use these to restate findings without overselling them.

- "This paper shows that ..."
- "Taken together, the results suggest that ..."
- "The evidence indicates that ..."
- "The contribution of the paper is ..."
- "These findings have implications for ..."
- "At the same time, several limitations remain."
- "First, ..."
- "Second, ..."
- "Future work could examine ..."
- "The broader implication is not that ..., but rather that ..."

### Conclusion-discipline reminder

- do not make stronger claims in the conclusion than in the results
- separate evidence from policy extrapolation

---

## Claim-Strength Patterns

Use these to match language to design strength.

### Stronger, when the design clearly supports it

- "increases"
- "reduces"
- "leads to"
- "causal effect"

### More cautious, when the design is weaker or evidence is indirect

- "is associated with"
- "is linked to"
- "is consistent with"
- "suggests"
- "appears to"
- "may operate through"

Choose the weaker formulation when in doubt.

---

## Working Rule

A good economics writing pattern should help the reader answer:

1. what is the claim?  
2. what design supports it?  
3. how strong is the evidence?  
4. what is the economic meaning?  
5. what remains uncertain?  

If a sentence sounds impressive but makes those questions harder to answer, it is probably too vague or too strong.
