# Verified Citation Workflow for Economics and Agricultural Economics Papers

## Principle

Never cite a paper, working paper, institutional report, policy document, or dataset description that you cannot verify.

Verification is not only about whether the source exists.  
It is also about whether:

- the metadata are correct
- the version is correct
- the cited claim actually matches the source
- the source is the substantively right one to cite

---

## Preferred Verification Order

Use sources in this order unless the case clearly requires otherwise:

1. DOI landing page or journal publisher page
2. publisher metadata page
3. Crossref record for metadata cross-check
4. RePEc / IDEAS / National Bureau of Economic Research / AEA or field-journal listing
5. Google Scholar as a discovery aid, not as the final authority

For agricultural economics and related field journals, also prioritize the journal’s own page when available.

Do **not** use Google Scholar as the sole source of truth.

---

## Published Paper Discipline

When citing published papers:

- verify the final published title
- verify the final journal venue
- verify publication year, volume, issue, and page range when relevant
- verify DOI from the publisher or DOI landing page
- use the published version when it is substantively the correct source

Default rule:

- prefer the published version over earlier working-paper versions unless there is a specific reason not to

---

## Working Paper Discipline

When citing working papers:

- record the series and number when available
- note if there is a later journal version
- do not cite both versions as if they were different papers unless there is a reason
- check whether the title, author order, or year changed across versions
- make sure the version cited is the version that actually contains the claim you rely on

Common working-paper sources include:

- `National Bureau of Economic Research Working Paper`
- `RePEc`
- `IDEAS`
- `SSRN`
- other recognized series when relevant

---

## What To Check

For every citation, check:

- author list
- year
- title
- venue or series
- DOI if published
- working paper number if unpublished
- whether the published version and working-paper version are being confused
- whether the cited claim is actually supported by the cited source

When the citation is substantively important, also check:

- publication status
- whether the paper is the final version
- whether the paper provides direct evidence or only indirect background
- whether the cited sentence matches the paper’s actual result, mechanism, or institutional discussion

---

## Claim-Source Match Rule

Do not stop at metadata verification.

Also verify:

- does the cited paper actually support the sentence in the manuscript?
- is the cited result in that exact version?
- is the paper direct evidence, indirect support, or only background context?

If a sentence relies on a specific mechanism, estimate, institutional fact, or identification argument, verify that exact point rather than assuming the paper is close enough.

---

## Policy, Dataset, and Institutional Citation Rule

For policy facts, institutional details, and dataset descriptions:

- prefer primary official sources when possible
- use academic papers to support interpretation, precedent, or broader context
- do not use a secondary paper when the correct source should be an official policy document or dataset documentation
- verify dates, names, rollout timing, and official definitions carefully

Examples of appropriate primary sources include:

- policy documents
- official survey documentation
- statistical yearbooks
- government or agency releases
- dataset user guides

---

## Common Failures

- citing an early working-paper title after the paper is published under a new title
- mixing journal and working-paper years
- using SSRN, NBER, and journal versions inconsistently
- citing a secondary summary instead of the source paper
- citing a published paper when the specific result exists only in the working-paper version
- citing a paper that is relevant in topic but does not support the exact claim
- citing a policy fact from a journal article when an official source should be cited instead

---

## Minimum Rule

Use at least two independent sources when the citation is central to the paper’s contribution, identification argument, or factual claim.

For especially important citations, the two-source rule should ideally include:

- one primary source for metadata or official documentation
- one independent cross-check source

---

## Output Discipline

When finalizing citations, record clearly:

- whether the cited version is published or unpublished
- whether a later journal version exists
- the correct full journal name or working-paper series
- DOI if published
- working-paper number if unpublished
- any note needed for why this version, rather than another version, was chosen

Default style rule:

- use full journal names unless a journal-specific bibliography style explicitly requires abbreviations

---

## Working Rule

Before keeping a citation in the manuscript, ask:

1. does this source exist in the cited version?  
2. are the metadata correct?  
3. is this the right version to cite?  
4. does it actually support the claim I am making?  
5. should this be a primary source instead?  

If any answer is uncertain, do not finalize the citation yet.
