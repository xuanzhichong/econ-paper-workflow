# Writing Guide for Empirical Economics and Agricultural Economics Papers

## Core Standard

The reader should be able to answer six questions quickly:

1. what is the question?
2. why does it matter?
3. what does the literature already know?
4. what identifies the effect?
5. where is the evidence?
6. how strong is the claim relative to the design?

A strong paper is not just interesting.  
It is legible, design-aware, and proportionate in its claims.

---

## Introduction

The introduction should let the reader understand, within a few paragraphs:

- the economic or policy motivation
- what the existing literature already establishes
- what remains unresolved
- the source of identifying variation
- the headline findings
- the contribution stated without hype
- the paper roadmap

### Writing rule

The introduction should promise only what the design can support.

Good introduction moves include:

- motivate the question clearly
- explain why the literature is incomplete, not just large
- preview the empirical leverage
- state the contribution precisely rather than promotionally

---

## Institutional Background / Context

When the paper relies on a policy, rule change, or institutional setting, this section should:

- explain the setting, policy, market, or eligibility rule
- describe timing, rollout, or assignment
- provide only the context needed to understand the design
- clarify why the setting generates useful empirical variation

### Writing rule

Do not turn this section into a general background essay.  
It should support identification, not distract from it.

---

## Data

The data section should let another researcher understand:

- where the data come from
- what the unit of observation is
- what the sample is
- what the sample window is
- how key variables are constructed
- what restrictions or exclusions are applied
- what cannot be observed
- what transformations, deflation, winsorization, or trimming rules are used

### Writing rule

The data section should make the estimation sample and the key variable definitions reconstructable, even if full detail is deferred to the appendix.

---

## Empirical Strategy

Do not hide the design inside generic methods language.

State:

- the estimating equation or design
- the source of identifying variation
- FE and controls
- clustering choice
- weights if used
- assumptions
- threats to identification
- why the diagnostics speak to those assumptions
- omitted period or normalization if relevant
- estimator-specific choices when relevant

### Writing rule

The empirical strategy section should make the logic of the design explicit before the reader reaches the main estimates.

---

## Results

Write around the table sequence.

For each main result:

- identify the relevant table or figure
- interpret the sign and magnitude
- explain what changes across columns
- distinguish statistical precision from economic meaning
- separate main effect, mechanism, and robustness
- keep supporting material in later sections or the appendix
- signpost appendix material when it matters for interpretation

### Writing rule

The results section should be organized by identification logic, not by accumulation of output.

A good internal sequence is usually:

1. baseline estimates  
2. identification support or dynamic evidence  
3. robustness tied to threats  
4. heterogeneity and mechanism as supporting evidence  

---

## Mechanism and Heterogeneity

Use these sections to support interpretation, not to replace the main result.

Mechanism and heterogeneity sections should:

- be theory-motivated
- remain narrower than the main claim
- distinguish supportive evidence from separately identified evidence
- avoid making subgroup or mediation results sound structurally definitive when they are not

### Writing rule

Mechanism evidence should usually be written as **consistent with** or **suggestive of**, unless the design truly supports more.

---

## Robustness

A robustness section should test the design’s weak spots, not generate a random appendix.

Good robustness writing should:

- identify the threat first
- explain the check
- report what changes
- state whether the substantive conclusion survives

### Writing rule

Do not write “the results are robust to many specifications” unless the paragraph clearly states what threat each specification addresses.

---

## Conclusion

The conclusion should:

- restate the main finding at the right level of strength
- summarize the contribution
- distinguish evidence from policy extrapolation
- acknowledge limitations honestly
- state broader implications without overselling them

### Writing rule

Do not make stronger claims in the conclusion than in the results.

---

## Tone

- avoid benchmark language
- avoid claiming certainty beyond the design
- keep mechanism evidence narrower than the main result
- keep policy language conditional when external validity is limited
- keep novelty claims precise rather than inflated
- keep robustness claims tied to threats
- keep causal language aligned with identification strength

### Preferred logic

- clear rather than flashy
- specific rather than vague
- disciplined rather than promotional
- transparent rather than defensive

---

## Working Rule

A well-written empirical economics paper should let a careful reader answer quickly:

1. what is identified?  
2. on what sample?  
3. under what assumptions?  
4. where is the main evidence?  
5. how large is the effect in economic terms?  
6. what remains uncertain?  

If those questions are hard to answer, the writing is not yet doing enough work for the design.
