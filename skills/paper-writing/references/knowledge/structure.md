# Structure Patterns for Empirical Economics and Agricultural Economics Papers

Use this note as a structure reference for empirical economics and agricultural economics papers.  
These patterns are meant to guide manuscript organization, not to force a rigid template.

## Introduction Pattern

A strong introduction usually does the following:

1. motivate the research question and explain its economic or policy relevance
2. explain what the existing literature already knows
3. identify what remains unresolved, unclear, or weakly identified
4. state the paper’s design, data, and empirical leverage
5. summarize the main finding at an appropriate level of caution
6. state the paper’s contribution relative to the literature
7. briefly preview the remainder of the paper

### Common writing logic
- question -> importance -> literature limit -> design -> main finding -> contribution -> roadmap

---

## Institutional Background / Context Pattern

When the paper relies on a policy, program, market setting, or institutional feature, this section usually does the following:

1. describe the institutional or policy setting
2. explain timing, eligibility, rollout, or treatment assignment
3. clarify why the setting generates useful identifying variation
4. distinguish policy facts from interpretation
5. provide only the background needed for the empirical design

### Common writing logic
- setting -> rules -> timing -> source of variation -> relevance for identification

---

## Data Pattern

A strong data section usually does the following:

1. introduce the data source(s)
2. define the unit of analysis and sample period
3. explain sample construction and restrictions
4. define treatment, outcome, and main control variables
5. describe key descriptive statistics
6. note important measurement limits or missing-data rules

### Common writing logic
- data source -> sample -> variables -> summary statistics -> limitations

---

## Empirical Strategy Pattern

A strong empirical strategy section usually does the following:

1. define the treatment, outcome, and estimating sample
2. explain the identifying variation or assignment mechanism
3. present the baseline specification
4. state the control variables, fixed effects, clustering, and weights if relevant
5. clarify the identifying assumptions
6. discuss main threats to identification
7. explain which diagnostics, robustness checks, or alternative specifications will address those threats

### Common writing logic
- estimand -> variation -> specification -> assumptions -> threats -> validation plan

---

## Results Pattern

A strong results section usually follows a sequence like this:

1. present the baseline results
2. interpret sign, magnitude, and economic meaning
3. discuss identification diagnostics or design-validity evidence when relevant
4. present heterogeneity or mechanism results if they are theory-motivated
5. distinguish supporting evidence from headline findings
6. direct the reader to appendix material for supplementary results when needed

### Common writing logic
- baseline finding -> interpretation -> design support -> extensions -> limits

### Important discipline rule
- do not let mechanism or heterogeneity evidence outrun the main design
- do not present robustness checks as entirely new headline findings

---

## Robustness Pattern

Robustness material usually does the following:

1. revisit the main identifying concern
2. show alternative specifications or samples tied to that concern
3. test sensitivity to definitions, controls, bandwidths, timing, or estimation choices when relevant
4. clarify whether the main conclusion changes
5. keep robustness tied to the design rather than turning it into a second results section

### Common writing logic
- threat -> check -> result -> implication for credibility

---

## Appendix Pattern

A strong appendix usually contains material that supports the paper without displacing core design logic from the main text.

Common appendix components include:

1. variable construction detail
2. sample construction, attrition, or coding detail
3. supplementary specifications
4. placebo or falsification tests
5. additional heterogeneity or mechanism tables
6. extra institutional detail not essential to the main flow
7. additional figures, diagnostics, or event-study plots
8. replication notes, data-access notes, or implementation detail when relevant

### Appendix rule
- move supporting detail to the appendix
- do not hide critical identification logic or indispensable sample definitions there

---

## Common Main-Text Flow

A common full-paper structure is:

1. Introduction
2. Institutional Background / Context
3. Data
4. Empirical Strategy
5. Main Results
6. Mechanisms / Heterogeneity
7. Robustness
8. Conclusion
9. Appendix

This is a common pattern, not a mandatory formula.  
Some papers combine or rename sections depending on journal style and paper length.

---

## Optional Module Rules

### Mechanism
Use a separate mechanism section when:
- the mechanism is central to the paper’s contribution
- the evidence is more than a brief extension
- the mechanism analysis is clearly distinguishable from the baseline result

### Heterogeneity
Use a separate heterogeneity section when:
- subgroup differences are theoretically motivated
- the subgroup structure is central to interpretation
- the analysis is substantial enough to merit its own subsection

### Robustness in Appendix
Move robustness mostly to the appendix when:
- the checks are numerous but routine
- the main text would become overloaded
- the main result is already well established in the body text

---

## Writing Discipline Reminders

- the structure should follow the identification logic, not just journal convention
- the introduction should promise only what the design can support
- the results section should separate headline findings from supporting checks
- the appendix should support the paper, not rescue missing core logic
- contribution claims should match what the paper actually identifies
