# Tone Guidelines for Economics and Agricultural Economics Response Letters

The goal is to sound professional, transparent, and calm.

These tone guidelines are for journal `reviewer response` and `R&R` letters in empirical economics and agricultural economics.  
They are not for conference responses and not for defensive argument.

A strong tone does **not** mean sounding submissive.  
It means sounding:

- respectful
- direct
- evidence-based
- specific about manuscript changes
- proportionate to what was actually revised

---

## Core Principle

The best tone in a response letter is:

- respectful in form
- clear in logic
- restrained in claims
- honest about limits

The goal is not to “win” the exchange.  
The goal is to make the reviewer and editor believe that:

1. you understood the concern  
2. you took it seriously  
3. you addressed it substantively  
4. you are not overstating what changed  

---

## Preferred Tone

Use a tone that is:

- respectful
- direct
- evidence-based
- specific about manuscript changes
- calm when agreeing
- calm when disagreeing
- honest about what was and was not implemented

A strong response should sound like a careful scholar, not like a litigator, marketer, or apologetic student.

---

## Tone vs. Stance

Keep tone and stance separate.

Your **tone** should remain professional regardless of stance.  
Your **stance** may differ across comments:

- agree and revise
- clarify and revise exposition
- clarify without revising
- partially accommodate
- respectfully decline

Do not confuse politeness with agreement.  
You can be polite while declining a request.

---

## Good Phrases

### General acknowledgement

- "We thank the reviewer for this helpful comment."
- "We appreciate this constructive suggestion."
- "We thank the reviewer for raising this important concern."

### Agree and revise

- "We agree that the previous draft did not explain this clearly enough."
- "To address this concern, we now ..."
- "We added Table A.X and revised Section Y accordingly."
- "We have revised the manuscript to clarify ..."

### Clarify existing logic

- "Our interpretation remains unchanged, but we agree that the original exposition was too compressed."
- "We now explain this point more explicitly in ..."
- "We have revised the text to make the identifying logic clearer."

### Partial accommodation

- "We agree with the concern and have incorporated part of the requested analysis."
- "Specifically, we now provide ..."
- "We do not implement the full request because ..., and we now state this limitation directly."

### Respectful decline

- "We respectfully do not adopt this suggestion because ..."
- "Implementing this request would change the estimand / weaken identification / require unavailable data."
- "We instead clarify this boundary in the manuscript and add a short discussion of the tradeoff."

---

## Preferred Tone Moves

### 1. Be specific about what changed

Prefer:

- "We added Table A.X ..."
- "We revised Section 4.2 ..."
- "We now report the alternative specification in Appendix Table A5 ..."

Avoid:

- "We revised accordingly."
- "The manuscript has been improved."
- "This has been addressed."

### 2. Be transparent about limits

Prefer:

- "We cannot implement the exact request because ..."
- "The data do not allow us to ..."
- "This check reduces, but does not eliminate, the concern that ..."

Avoid:

- implying full resolution when only partial accommodation was possible
- using vague reassurance instead of explanation

### 3. Use cautious evidentiary language

Prefer:

- "This reduces the concern that ..."
- "These results are consistent with ..."
- "The evidence is suggestive rather than definitive."
- "The estimate remains similar in sign and magnitude, although precision falls."

Avoid:

- "This proves that ..."
- "This fully resolves ..."
- "This definitively establishes ..."

---

## Avoid

- "The reviewer is wrong."
- "Obviously"
- "Clearly the reviewer misunderstood."
- vague promises without a concrete manuscript change
- overstating what a new robustness check proves
- sounding irritated, sarcastic, or dismissive
- saying "we fully agree" when only part of the request was implemented
- claiming a manuscript revision when the response only clarifies the issue in the letter

---

## Economics-Specific Reminder

If the reviewer points to an identification or data concern, respond with:

- design logic
- new diagnostics or targeted checks
- sample clarification
- manuscript changes
- scope clarification when needed

Do **not** respond with rhetorical reassurance alone.

For example, do not rely only on phrases like:

- "We believe our design is sound."
- "We respectfully disagree."
- "This should not be a major concern."

Instead, show:

- what identifying assumption is at stake
- what was added or revised
- what remains limited
- where the revision appears in the manuscript

---

## Common Tone Errors

### Defensive tone

Problem:
- the response sounds irritated or combative

Better:
- acknowledge the concern, then answer directly and calmly

### Evasive tone

Problem:
- the response sounds polite but never actually answers the question

Better:
- state the concern in plain language and respond to the substance

### Over-accommodating tone

Problem:
- the response sounds too eager to agree, even when the request is only partly feasible

Better:
- be honest about what you can implement and what remains outside scope

### Inflated tone

Problem:
- the response exaggerates what a new check or revision has accomplished

Better:
- describe the contribution of the new material accurately and narrowly

---

## Working Rule

A well-toned response should let the reader feel that the authors are:

- serious
- careful
- transparent
- non-defensive
- trustworthy

If the response sounds polished but avoids saying what actually changed, the tone is not yet good enough.
